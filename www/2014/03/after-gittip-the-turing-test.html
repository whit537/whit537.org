My philosophy reading group, SAPS, is on the rocks; I just bowed out for the indefinite future. Gittip is coming up on its two-year anniversary. These two factors together have me thinking about my future in the academy, the second of three career foci that I’m advertising on my Gittip statement:

I am making the world better by tinkering with the economy, the academy, and the church.
  • economy: https://www.gittip.com/
  • academy: http://abstract.io/n
  • church: http://xmin.org/

I just reviewed My Goals, and it looks to me like this statement is in line. I’d like to clarify it in two ways. First, Gittip is about generosity. That’s the key word, despite the mission’s current wordiness. Second, I’m going to start exploring the Turing Test as a possible focus for the philosophical/academic part of my career.

I’ve been intrigued by the Turing Test for years, but it has felt out of reach. It still feels out of reach, but closer. For one, I just finished Philosophical Investigations and Cryptonomicon, so Turing and his milieu feel more like people to me now, parts of something that I also maybe could be a part of. Gittip’s success to date and my brushes with academic philosophy are also boosting my confidence.

What I like about the Turing Test is that it gives philosophy something to break itself against. As with Gittip, I’m not interested in idle chatter, I want to bring forth something real into the world. Abstract.io/n is not an end in itself. The point is to discover truth. The truth I am interested in discovering is the truth about what human experience consists in, what its possibilities and limits are. As Kant says, “[I]t must be possible [...] to attain certainty either concerning our knowledge or lack of knowledge of the objects [of metaphysics], or certainty concerning the ability or inability of reason to make judgments about these objects” (CPR, B22). Or, as Wittgenstein says, “The real discovery is the one that allows me to break off philosophizing when I want to.—The one that gives philosophy peace, so that it is no longer tormented by questions which bring itself in question” (PI, §133). I want to find Kant’s second certainty, Wittgenstein’s real discovery, and that means thoroughly charting conscious human experience. What surer sign of having understood human consciousness than replicating it in some fashion?

The current best instantiation of the Turing Test is the Loebner Prize. Turns out it’s rather colorful, and consequently ostracized from mainstream research. Could be a fun place to play, especially if I remain an outsider. However, there are rumblings that the mainstream is almost ready to revisit the Turing Test. Either way, I would expect to tackle the Turing Test in the same open and crowd-friendly way in which I’m approaching Gittip.

As I alluded to above and have hinted at in years past, I have some trouble identifying with mainstream society. For example, I’m pretty strongly turned off by the thought that any winner of the Loebner Prize will have simply paved the way for the next generation of mindless entertainment and war. Gittip is an attempt to create a mainstream society I can feel good about being a part of and donating my best ideas to. Our culture of laziness and violence is a demand-side problem. Gittip is about warming the collective heart of culture so we care less about bullshit (presumably the third part of my career will address this somehow, too, if I ever get there).

So what’s next? Here’s the beginnings of a reading list:

  • “Computing Machinery and Intelligence,” Turing’s original paper
  • Shieber contra Loebner, and Loebner’s response
  • “The Turing Test,” (SEP) and bibliography
  • “The Chinese Room Argument,” (SEP)
  • articles by Robert French
  • PhilPapers: search, and curated bibliography

Let’s see if this goes anywhere!
