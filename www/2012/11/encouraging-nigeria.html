Three months ago I left a comfortable full time job in order to build Gittip, a platform for sustainable crowd-funding. Since Gittip is funded on Gittip, I need some part-time work to pay the bills while Gittip gets off the ground. Thankfully, my former employer, YouGov, decided to keep me on as a contractor, but for a while there I was looking for freelance work (and thanks to everyone who offered!).

The most interesting lead I got was from a Nigerian government official. It’s a regrettable sign of the times that I was immediately suspicious, as is pretty much anyone else I mention this to here in the United States. For whatever reason, Nigeria is famously and disproportionately associated with Internet fraud. I engaged the conversation anyway. Long story short, this is legit. I wish I could take the job, but I need to stay focused on Gittip, and YouGov is the best way for me to do that. However, my Nigerian contact has expressed consternation at not being able to find good technology partners to work with, and I promised that I would try to help.

The Person

Dr. Muyiwa Gbadegesin earned his Ph.D. in neuroscience from Georgetown in the 90s, and, in February 2012, became the Commisioner for Health for Oyo State in western Nigeria. I’m embarrassed to have to establish his credentials for him, but here are his scientific publications:

  • Regional and Ontogenic Expression of the NMDA Receptor Subunit NR2D Protein in Rat Brain Using a Subunit-Specific Antibody
  • Hypoxia modulates nitric oxide-induced regulation of NMDA receptor currents and neuronal cell death
  • Nitroxyl anion regulation of the NMDA receptor
Here is his thesis advisor at Georgetown, and his Twitter account, and here are some news stories about his work in Oyo State:

  • OYSG Reshuffles Cabinet
  • It’s Strange, But We Have Fewer Than 100 Doctors In Oyo Hospitals - Commissioner
  • Oyo wages war against malaria
  • Oyo distributes treated mosquito nets

I have corresponded with Muyiwa since July over email, Twitter, and Skype, and I can vouch for him. He’s not a scammer. He’s a capable official with great intentions and a lot of energy looking to improve the health care system and raise the standard of living in his state. I would be proud to work with him.

The Project

Muyiwa has an expansive vision for using technology to improve delivery of health care in his state. Much of Oyo State is rural, and basic cell phones are prevalent even where smart phones or Internet access are not. Therefore we have been talking about using text messaging to improve information flow for his department. He’s particularly keen on using open source tools and contributing back to the open source community.

Here’s the latest part of our email exchange:

We had been looking at existing platforms like Ushahidi and FrontlineSMS. As far as I can tell, the technical challenge in either case is to collect inbound SMS messages. I started looking into SMS hosting services. Another option is to buy a GSM modem and put it in a server yourself. I believe Muyiwa has done this before, actually, for an election monitoring project. Once the modem is installed, you can apparently get at it with drivers like RubyGSM and pyGSM. There’s a REST server wrapping pyGSM called SlingshotSMS. I’m not sure what value Ushahidi and FronlineSMS add over a custom Rails or Django app that talks to the modem directly.

I did blink-test Muyiwa with some U.S.-normal consulting rates, and he was fine. We didn’t negotiate a final price though, and I’ve never billed him, so while I think it should be worth your while I can’t say for sure.

The Bigger Picture

It’s a shame that stereotypes about Nigerians hurt people like Muyiwa who are genuinely trying to do good. On the other hand, every positive encounter with the mainstream Internet shifts the incentive a little bit further away from fraud. If you are interested in solving interesting tech challenges to improve the health care system in Oyo State, while contributing to open source, making money, and chipping away at Nigerian fraud, then get in touch with Muyiwa and take the job I wish I could take.
