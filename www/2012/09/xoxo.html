XOXO was great because it subverted my personal perceptions of fame. There were a number of relatively well-known people at XOXO, and I recognized so relatively few of them that I could rarely tell whether the person I was about to introduce myself to was “famous” or not. This shined a light for me on my personal biases and hang-ups around fame.

One person I did recognize was Tim O’Reilly. I made a point to chase him down and awkwardly introduce myself and shove a business card in his hand. Blech. Sorry, Tim. Two hours later I happened to be in the right place at the right time to introduce Tim to Dan Harmon. This was right after Dan’s keynote, and I ended up tagging along when their group went out for drinks, where I met a bunch more people and had a good time. Eventually Tim left and Dan left, and I felt relieved, to be honest. I felt like I could let my, “Oh shit, I’m around famous people” guard down a bit. And I did, and I had an even better time with some great new friends discussing all sorts of interesting things like collective memory and the value of psychedelics and access to technology. And then the next day I discovered that Jenna Wortham has half a million Twitter followers.

I got to thinking, just how diverse is the XOXO crowd in terms of fame? So I scraped the list of Twitter screen names from the XOXO website, and used the Twitter API to get the data for following and followers (friends_count and followers_count in the API). Then I plotted the following chart, showing the log of the number of Twitter followers for each attendee along the X axis, and the log of the number they’re following along Y.

The thing I notice about this chart is that there are no clumps. It’s not like there were a bunch of big names, and then a mass of the rest of us. It looks really evenly distributed to me. It’s also interesting to see the fairly well-defined upper and lower bounds on the ratio of followers to following. In another context I’ve considered that ratio as a possible indicator of abuse, and if we can assume of XOXO attendees that they are not abusing Twitter (some of them, in fact, building it for us), then this plot seems to indicate that there might be something to that idea. I’ve started working on comparing XOXO to Twitter overall, using the ASU Twitter dataset, but that’s proving more difficult to compute (a pseudo-random sample of 500 users takes me about 40 minutes to compute with my current algorithm, and shows way too many users in the single digits on both axes: Twitter appears to be sparsely populated). It would also be interesting to do comparisons to other conferences, perhaps by mining Lanyrd.

In any case, fame is an axis of diversity that I don’t usually think of, and it looks like XOXO really nailed fame diversity. How? I think having open enrollment helped. Yes, XOXO was a high-demand event, due to the reputation of the organizers. The only reason most of us made it to XOXO was because we happened to be paying attention to Twitter at just the right moment, when the conference was first announced. Insofar as that’s true, it seems to indicate that how frequently one uses Twitter is independent of the number of followers and friends one has. (Of course, having money and time to spend on XOXO was also a factor.)

XOXO’s fame diversity makes me question my bias against exclusive events. I’ve had this negative impression of events like Foo Camp, Brooklyn Beta, Build and CodeMash as cliques of the well-connected. I had XOXO in the same box in other respects, so it’s surprising to me that well-connectedness was largely irrelevant there. While Twitter is only one measure of connectedness, it’d be interesting to see the data for these other conferences. Is Foo Camp less fame-diverse for being invite-only? Do the same people attend Brooklyn Beta every year? It’d also be fun to look at more general events like Aspen Ideas and even TED, if attendee data were available.

After XOXO I’m more likely to try to get in to exclusive events. Exclusivity, fame-bias, and cliquishness are separate concerns, and exclusivity does not necessarily imply the other two. Certainly, part of the value of XOXO was in its small, intimate size. Can it stay exclusive—that is, intimate—while remaining fame-diverse and not falling into cliquishness? We shall see. In the mean time, I have my eye on Brooklyn Beta Beyond.

So What?

To me, diversity is not an end in itself, it’s how to enrich conversation. Exploring the world and identifying challenges and meeting them together and celebrating our achievements makes life fun. The more and diverse people there are at the table, the more we can explore and see and solve. And while XOXO brought an amazing group of people together, it fell a bit short for me in terms of the depth of the conversation, mostly through my own fault.

One of my favorite conversations at XOXO was over lunch with Chad Dickerson, Chris Lamothe, Neil Kandalgaonkar, and Vince Maniago. I asked why Wikipedia isn’t better than it is. Well, it turns out that Wikipedia has some serious cultural and technical constraints it’s dealing with, and that mobile might be Wikipedia’s best chance at breaking with the past and making some bigger changes. But even then there is no absolute truth about what Wikipedia should be, and it falls to the messy languages of politics and organizational leadership to think about how to move Wikipedia from one place to another. I wish I’d pursued more substantive conversations like this at XOXO.

I loved Dan’s keynote because he laid bare this really big question about the role of money in “people connectors” (his phrase for mediators of culture such as newspapers, TV, and the Internet). I saw a contrast in Adam’s keynote, where he “frames his obsession with making and replicating movie props as a conversation with popular culture.” Okay, but what’s the content of the conversation? Adam’s keynote was fun, Dan’s was important (and, don’t get me wrong, hilarious). I actually ended up at drinks with Adam and Dan, among others, after Adam’s keynote, and I regret not having followed up on this, constipated as I was by fame.

This interest in deepening the conversation is also behind a recent Twitter exchange about the idea of inviting something like Kiva to be a part of the next iteration of XOXO. Okay, it’s first and foremost an art festival. That’s a gestalt I didn’t really take on board beforehand. And I do want to try Braid now. But threatening suicide over not finishing your video game? That’s an attitude to critique, not celebrate. The Internet is creatively disrupting a lot more than how video games and films about video games and music inspired by video games are getting made. In my view, we’re poised to turn a big chunk of the economy inside out. Let’s play video games, and then let’s deepen the conversation. That’s what I hope to contribute to at events like XOXO in the future.
