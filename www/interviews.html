Thanks for your interest! :-)

My policy is to only do open interviews, because I only want to do work that is open and transparent and collaborative, if I can at all help it. Doing private interviews feels to me like a waste of time, because it leads to duplication of effort. If we share our interview, however, then the next interested person can start that much further down field—just as you’re able to! :-)

Practically speaking, here’s what that means:

  • All my interviews are [un]licensed under CC0.
  • For video calls we’ll do a Google Hangout on Air. These are live-streamed on YouTube and archived there as well. We’ll schedule the call by Twitter or email and then I’ll send you a link to the Hangout at our appointed time.
  • For email interviews, I’ll respond by posting answers here on my blog (e.g.).
  • For podcasts and radio/TV shows or other such things, I’ll record my own version of the experience, and publish it immediately.

I should also say: I’ve found that real-time interviews need to be at least 45 minutes to be meaningful as conversations. If we spend less time than that, we don’t really get anywhere unexpected or interesting.

I love serendipity in conversation! I love diving deep, chasing rabbit holes, finding some unexpected insight together. Let’s do it!

Required Reading

If you’re approaching me as a member of the media, please start by reading “Turning Down TechCrunch” (May, 2013). That was my first real experience with media attention, and it was formative for me.

Please also read “Crowdsource your salary! An economy built on love,” by Andrew Leonard, and (for bonus points), watch our hour-long interview. That’s the best of the interviews I’ve done so far.

You can find further material on this YouTube playlist, and linked from the “Turning Down TechCrunch” piece.

P.S.

Some people think I’m narcissitic to have a policy like this. I don’t want to dismiss this criticism out of hand. I’ll tell you what: I’m making this up as I go along. We all have to manage our time somehow, and part of how I manage my time is only doing open interviews. My ideal of open interviews has the happy pragmatic side-effect of limiting the number of interview requests I get, and of ensuring that the iterviews I do do are available for the next interested person who comes along.

I also genuinely believe that the best thing for me to do is to do my thing, with nary a care for what you’re going to write about me. Your customer is your audience. I’m not your customer, because I can reach my own audience directly via Twitter, etc.

Okay! I look forward to hearing from you. :-)
