Three years ago, three friends and I started a philosophy reading group called the Sewickley Area Philosophical Society (SAPS). Our first major project was reading Immanuel Kant’s Critique of Pure Reason.

The Sewickley Area Philosophical Society (SAPS) upon completion of Immanuel Kant’s Critique of Pure Reason, November 13, 2010. Left to right: Jeff Kearns, Chad Whitacre (myself), Earl Johns, and Brandon Smith.

I had started the so-called “first critique” a year prior on my friend Jeremy Sanderson’s advice in response to a blog post. I also considered taking a Kant class from Stephen Engstrom at the University of Pittsburgh. Well, I ended up taking (half of) a programming class instead, but a few months ago I chanced to get to know one of Engstrom’s grad students, Tyke Nunez, and he invited me to visit a grad seminar on Kant’s Critique of Judgement given by visiting professor Andrew Chignell. It turns out that Andrew graduated from Wheaton the year I arrived there, and I knew his brother. Andrew was very gracious to let me visit his seminar.

Finally, then, after four years, I’ve encountered two of Kant’s major critical works (along with a number of other books), and I’ve had an experience of academic philosophy. Honestly, it’s not where I need to be right now. I’m not in a position to switch careers (family, mortgage, momentum elsewhere), and visiting Andrew’s class—for which I’m grateful—made it clear that my trying to participate as an outsider is untenable. I’m too distant geographically and professionally for it to make sense for me to continue to impose there. Instead, I’ve rejoined the SAPS after two years apart. I had stepped away after the first critique; the group continued apace. My first book back is Alasdair MacIntyre’s After Virtue.

I would say that, philosophically, the past four years have been both encouraging and humbling. I’m still committed to the philosophy of abstraction, but coming up with a personal, idiosyncratic language is one thing, and establishing that language in the cultural consciousness is another. There was an extreme example of this recently, when a top mathematician emerged after two decades of isolation with a solution to one of math’s toughest problems; it’s so arcane that it will take years more for the rest of the math community to even begin to understand it. I think abstraction has legs. Whether it falls to me to bring it forth into the world remains to be seen. I console myself that if it’s essentially true, something like it is bound to come to light eventually, one way or another.

I see myself a few years into an extended period of listening and learning with regard to philosophy, during which I expect to continue doing much more reading than writing. Writing is a lot of work, and my priority right now is launching Gittip. Hopefully one day I’ll wake up and find that I’ve got a solid foundation laid and a chance to do something with it. Until then, discussions with SAPS and other friends and occasional blog posts will suffice.

So What Am I Thinking About?

I’m thinking about evolution. I’m reading The Selfish Gene by Richard Dawkins. It’s the book that launched his career in the 70s. More recently he’s become renowned for his atheism, and he’s been something of a bogeyman for me personally (see here and here). He does have a religious chip on his shoulder in Selfish Gene, clearer to see in hindsight. I use the language of memes, which he coins at the end of this book, so I’m looking forward to reading that. So far he seems to be confused about the difference between efficient and final causes, but we shall see. Genetics itself is fascinating. Dawkins admits throughout that, this being a popular-level book, his language is imprecise and metaphorical. This makes me want to go deeper. How tight is the connection really between genes and behavior? What does the math look like? Evolution seems to me to be something of a catch-all theory that totally encompasses the past by reference to the present, but what does it say about the future? I have this brainstorm that other sciences could be reformulated as limiting cases of evolution. Next stops on this train are Lewontin’s “Units of Selection,” Dewey’s “Influence of Darwin on Philosophy,” and Darwin’s Origin of Species itself.

I’m thinking some about miracles, mostly because of Christmas. Reading The Selfish Gene while celebrating the Virgin Birth had the ironic effect of strengthening my faith in that particular miracle. I don’t think much of interventionist interpretations of the miraculous, but I’m fairly open to thinking that really weird stuff very occassionally happens. Evolution actually depends on it: random mutation. The two books on my radar here are those by C.S. Lewis and Craig Keener. But the really interesting thing to me is the interplay between miracles and culture. Nothing could be more dramatic and life-altering if experienced personally, and yet using belief in miracles as a litmus test for group membership is so ... fraught.

I’m thinking about the Gospels. I published my own edition so I could read them the way I wanted. Now to read them. :-) I’m motivated here by the desire to teach my kids, so I’d better get moving! I envision a curriculum characterized by a comprehensive analysis. I want to say to my kids things like, “There are 26 distinct miracles recorded in the Gospels, and 22 of them are healings. Can you name the other four?” I made up those numbers, but you get the idea.

I’m thinking about Islam. I read a translation of the Koran a while back, and now we’ve gotten to know a couple from Saudi Arabia. They’re from Mecca, actually. It’s fascinating to start to see the world through their eyes.

I’m thinking about emotion and morality. I’m wondering about a moral theory grounded in following. I’m interested to grant that value judgements are essentially subjective (contra MacIntyre, most immediately), and then look at morality as allegiance. The things to tease out here are the interplay between group and individual, and especially the way that individuals lead and follow. This is deep and rich, and touches on many things. I’d like to revisit The Hero with a Thousand Faces for this. I also have a book on my shelf called Self, by Richard Sorabji; I don’t know if it directly relates here but I’d like to get to it. Certainly, besides ethics, there’s whole fields of anthropology and psychology and cultural criticism that could be fruitfully engaged with this project in mind.

I’m also still thinking about abstraction. There’s a story to be told about the early moderns, and how abstraction reconciles their divergent metaphysics. Kant comes close, but the tentative story with Kant is how his presuppositions, especially his philosophy of science and his logic, constrain him. There are three books on Kant’s table of judgments that would be the next step for me there. I’ve also been introduced by Martha Lindeman to the field of cognitive psychology, which seems to be situated between neuroscience and philosophy. I’m eager to explore the body of work there on category formation. I intuit that experimental philosophy, which is on the edge of my radar, has things to say here as well.

I have one more chapter left in a symbolic logic workbook. I should get around to Parts one of these days. I finished a first pass through Plato’s Republic, and, while I need to read his second critique, I’m far enough along with Kant that I’m ready to hit some other big names: Aristotle and Wittgenstein. I’d also like to experience the presocratics at the root of the Western tradition; Nietzsche and Hegel, too.

But now I’m really stealing time from Gittip, unfortunately, and I have to go. I may not be posting much about philosophy this year, but don’t worry, I’ll be reading.
