Full disclosure: Balanced is a key partner for my business, Gittip.

A while back I told Jareau Wade, Balanced co-founder in charge of business development, that I thought their real-time merchant underwriting was an underappreciated feature. Now he's asked me to say more about that.

I see online payment processing as falling into two broad categories: e-wallet and white-label. PayPal is the incumbent e-wallet, followed by Google Checkout, Amazon Payments, etc. For this class of products the payment processing brand is consumer-facing. For the white-label class of products, the payment processing vendor is behind the scenes, and the merchant or marketplace is the consumer-facing brand. There's also offline payment processing, where Square seems to be doing well, and of course there's bitcoin, which for our purposes here is just another currency.

I think of the growth of white-label payments in terms of three phases.

Phase I was about offering online credit card processing at all. Basically, this meant putting paper forms online. You still had all the crap to deal with—the arcane fee structures, the negotiations, the fine print, clunky or non-existent APIs and dashboards. It was better than nothing, but it was begging to be optimized. When I think of Phase I, I think of Authorize.net.

Phase II was about optimizing the developer experience for credit card processing. Paul Graham points out that the inefficiences of Phase I were a business opportunity hiding in plain site, and that Stripe saw it. Stripe cut out all the crap and offered a straight-forward fee structure with a gorgeous dashboard and API, and they won Phase II. (Incidentally, YCombinator funded both Stripe and Balanced, as well as WePay. Jareau has suggested that YC host a royal rumble of sorts, and if you want to see that like I do, you should add a +1 over on this GitHub issue.)

Now, as a personal aside, I spent the aughts not doing ecommerce. I built a lot of websites but never got over the hump of inefficiency represented by the Phase I processors. I sort of woke up a year or two ago in a nice, comfy corporate job, wondering what the heck had happened to all of my start-up ambitions (easy answer: four kids and a mortgage, that's what!). I had heard buzz around Phase II processors, and I decided to go back to basics and build the minimum viable web business: a page where people could enter a dollar amount and credit card info and it would deposit money in my bank account. It worked. I convinced a friend to give me a dollar through that site and that is the first dollar I ever made on the web.

The vendor I used was a Phase II contender, FeeFighters. They promised easy pricing and they did have a nice dashboard and API as well as great support, but they didn't hide the underlying merchant account well enough and I ended up getting burned on fees. It actually got worse. My minimum viable business developed into a personal finance app called IHasAMoney, which didn't really go anywhere, but then I started Gittip, which did get some traction. By then FeeFighters had gotten acquired by GroupOn and I got caught in acquisition hiccups. Two weeks into Gittip I had to scramble to find another vendor, and by then Stripe was the obvious choice. Unfortunately, a few weeks after that, Stripe had to ask me to leave because I was violating their terms of service. That's when I started to realize that credit card processing is not the only piece of the payments puzzle.

Phase III takes credit card processing as a solved problem, and pushes further. That's what Balanced does. Stripe went depth-first: they took one piece of the puzzle, credit card processing, and totally nailed it. Balanced went breadth-first: they've spent a few years now laying the foundation for a whole suite of payment processing products, and it's starting to pay off. Yes, you can process credit cards on Balanced, with a decent API and dashboard and pricing comparable to Stripe's. But you can also do next-day and in some cases same-day bank transfers in both directions with Balanced. You can also hold money in escrow with Balanced, with free transfers to and from your own bank account. Stripe has bolted on some marketplace features, but they don't compare: with Stripe it takes seven days for merchants to receive funds.

Real-time merchant underwriting is the really killer feature that Balanced has that, as far as I'm aware, no-one else does. With Stripe (etc.) your users have to sign up for a Stripe account on Stripe's website before you can move money on their behalf. Balanced makes this an API call. Balanced literally has an API call for Underwriting an Individual and another one for Underwriting a Business. I would be quite interested to learn of another payments provider with parallel API calls. I double-checked Stripe and WePay, and I'm not seeing it if it's there. What this means is that Balanced isn't just for merchants, it's also for marketplaces. That opens up a whole new dimension in the payments space.

Yes, Stripe solved payments for merchants. Balanced picks up from there, and goes on to solve payments for entire marketplaces. Dunno about you, but I'd call that one heck of a schlep. The schlep isn't over, of course. For example, Balanced hasn't expanded beyond the United States yet. You should add a +1 on this GitHub issue if that's something you want to see. And if you're doing a startup in the U.S. and you're looking for a three-dimensional, third-generation online payments provider, you should check out Balanced.
